# Causal Survival Analysis in Platform Trials with Non-Concurrent Controls 

## Antonio D’Alessandro, Samrachana Adhikari, and Michele Santacatterina ∗

## Division of Biostatistics, Department of Population Health, New York University School of Medicine, New York, NY, 10016 

Abstract 

Platform trials allow treatment arms to enter and exit over time while maintain-ing a shared control arm, yielding concurrent and non-concurrent controls (NCC). Pooling NCC is often motivated as a strategy to improve statistical efficiency, but it is unclear which estimand is targeted, what assumptions justify identification and estimation, and when precision gains are achievable; these questions are further com-plicated by time-to-event/survival data. Motivated by the Adaptive COVID-19 Treat-ment Trial (ACTT) platform trial with time to recovery as the primary endpoint, we develop an estimand-first causal survival framework targeting the treatment-specific counterfactual survival curve in the concurrent population and the corresponding functionals including the concurrent restricted mean survival time (RMST). We give nonparametric identification results and formalize conditions that justify pooling us-ing NCC. We study covariate-adjusted outcome-regression (OR) and doubly robust (DR) estimators for the concurrent RMST, comparing concurrent-only versions to pooled-control versions. Pooling improves precision for OR estimators only when the pooling assumption holds and parametric hazard models are correctly specified; oth-erwise, pooling can induce bias. Keywords: Causal survival estimands; Doubly robust estimation; Non-concurrent controls; Platform trials; Restricted mean survival time 21 Introduction 

A platform trial is an adaptive multi-arm experimental design that allows for the simulta-neous evaluation of multiple treatments for a single disease (Berry et al. 2015). Traditional randomized controlled trials (RCTs) feature parallel arms which remain fixed by design through the duration of the experiment, while platform designs allow different treatment arms to enter and exit at distinct times, while also maintaining a shared control arm through the end of the study. This shared control arm is composed of concurrent controls , subjects who entered the trial concurrent with the treatment of interest and could have been ran-domized to the treatment of interest or control arm, and non-concurrent controls (NCC), subjects who entered the trial when the treatment of interest was unavailable and had zero probability of being assigned to the treatment of interest. This design has seen widespread use during the COVID-19 pandemic (Hayward et al. 2021). For example, the Adaptive COVID-19 Treatment Trial (ACTT) (Kalil et al. 2021) evaluated multiple regimens across stages, with remdesivir serving as a shared arm across ACTT-1 and ACTT-2. Time-to-event endpoints are common in platform trials and require methods that ac-commodate censoring. Although analyses often rely on the Cox proportional hazards model (Cox 1972), the hazard ratio targets an instantaneous risk-set contrast that may not cor-respond to a clinically meaningful causal effect on survival time (Hern´ an 2010, Stensrud et al. 2019). This motivates complementary estimands such as the restricted mean survival time (RMST), which summarizes average survival up to a clinically relevant horizon and yields interpretable contrasts between treatment arms (Royston & Parmar 2013). While platform trials like the ACTT offer increased flexibility, they also create an ap-parent opportunity to increase precision by pooling earlier-stage control patients with con-current controls for a given treatment comparison. However, it is not generally clear when 3such pooling is valid, what causal quantity is targeted, or how pooling should be imple-mented for time-to-event endpoints in the presence of censoring. This raises three practical 

questions: 1) how should causal survival estimands be defined when controls are partly non-concurrent; 2) under what assumptions are these estimands identifiable and estimable; and 3) when, if at all, does incorporating NCC improve precision for the concurrent treatment comparison? Answering these questions requires thinking carefully about how calendar time can enter the data-generating process in a platform trial. When the distribution of baseline covariates change over the enrollment period, na¨ ıvely pooling NCC and concurrent controls can bias estimated treatment effects; this is commonly referred to as “time drift” or “temporal drift”. Proposed solutions to deal with this bias include test-then-pool proce-dures (Viele et al. 2014), regression adjustment (Saville et al. 2022), propensity weighting (Chen et al. 2020), and robust borrowing approaches (Kaizer et al. 2018, Wang et al. 2023). While these approaches enable the use of NCC, the causal target is often implicit and the estimand can be tied closely to a chosen modeling strategy (e.g., a particular regression coefficient or hazard ratio) rather than stated upfront as a clinically meaningful causal contrast and research question. This emphasis on specifying the scientific question first is central to the estimand-first framework advocated by the FDA (FDA 2021) and the ICH E9(R1) guidance (ICH 2017). Recent reviews note that, despite substantial methodological development, there remains a need for a unified estimand-first framework for platform trials that clearly links causal questions, identifying assumptions, and estimation strategies, including for time-to-event endpoints (Collignon et al. 2022, Koenig et al. 2024). Motivated by the ACTT platform trial with time to recovery as the primary endpoint, this paper addresses the practical questions raised above using an estimand-first framework. We give nonparametric identification results, formalize conditions for pooling NCC, and study covariate-adjusted outcome-regression and doubly robust estimators. Pooling NCC 4improves precision for outcome-regression estimators only under valid pooling assumptions and correct parametric hazard specification; otherwise it can induce bias. When treatment availability is deterministic in entry time, pooling NCC gives no efficiency gain for the dou-bly robust estimator. Overall, the most robust strategy is to target concurrent estimands and use covariate-adjusted doubly robust estimation with concurrent controls only. An ACTT application supports these findings. 

# 2 Additional Related Work 

Platform trials extend traditional parallel-arm designs through features such as shared control arms and adaptive treatment entry and removal (Berry & Saville 2016, Coalition 2019). Methodological development continues from both Bayesian and frequentist per-spectives (Mu et al. 2024, Greenstreet et al. 2025), with recent reviews documenting the diversity of design characteristics and analytic strategies (Massonnaud et al. 2025). A cen-tral statistical question concerns how best to leverage shared or pooled control information. Borrowing of control data, including from external sources, has a long history (Viele et al. 2014), and its potential efficiency gains in platform settings have been studied in Huang & Luedtke (2023) and Santacatterina et al. (2025); see Roig et al. (2023) for a review. For time-to-event endpoints, both frequentist and Bayesian approaches exploit shared controls. Frequentist proposals build on multi-arm multi-stage survival designs using stan-dard time-to-event methods under proportional hazards working models (Royston et al. 2011). Bayesian approaches include adaptive platform designs such as MIDAS (Yuan et al. 2016) and commensurate or MAP-style borrowing strategies for censored outcomes (Roy-choudhury & Neuenschwander 2020). Applications include REMAP-CAP (REMAP-CAP Investigators 2021) and the HEALEY ALS Platform Trial (HEALEY ALS Writing Com-5mittee 2025). Across these strands, inference is typically framed in terms of model-based parameters (e.g., hazard ratios or borrowing parameters), underscoring the importance of clearly specifying the causal estimand. We focus on estimating causal survival contrasts in platform trials using the restricted mean survival time (RMST), which offers interpreta-tional advantages over hazard ratios (Hasegawa et al. 2020); see Kloecker et al. (2020) for applied illustrations. Our estimators rely on semiparametric efficiency theory and double robustness (Kennedy et al. 2021, Hines et al. 2021), building on weighted estimating-equation approaches originally developed in Robins et al. (1994).

## 4.1 Restricted mean survival time 

The restricted mean survival time (RMST) is a novel alternative measure of time-to-event quantities in survival analysis. The RMST represents the area under the survival curve from time 0 to a given cut-off, in this work denoted τ . The interpretation is straight forward, the RMST is the average time-to-event during the time period between 0 and τ

(Hasegawa et al. 2020). Contrasts such as the difference in restricted mean survival time thus represent the difference in the average time-to-event between two specified groups such as treatment vs. control or new treatment vs. standard of care. In addition to the easy interpretation, the RMST and related contrasts remain valid even when the proportional hazards assumption does not hold (Royston & Parmar 2013), a common but often tenuous assumption in medical studies. Given the advantages of the RMST (and its contrasts), the causal estimand of interest in this work will be the difference in restricted mean survival time among the population of concurrent control subjects. ## 4.2 Other survival contrasts 

In this paper we focus on difference in restricted mean survival time, which as shown in Definition 4.2, is a linear function of θ(a, t ), however other contrasts may be constructed (Westling et al. 2024). For example, following the analysis conducted in the ACTT study, one may consider the recovery ratio θ(0 , t )/θ (1 , t ). Additionally, other end points common in survival analysis such as: the survival ratio θ(1 , t )/θ (0 , t ), risk difference {1 − θ(1 , t )} − {1 − θ(0 , t )} or risk-ratio {1 − θ(1 , t )}/{1 − θ(0 , t )} may be calculated depending on the scientific question of interest.

## 5.2 Doubly robust estimators 

So called doubly robust (DR) estimators provided consistent treatment effect estimates by combining outcome regression and inverse probability weights. DR estimators are prefer-able to outcome regression as the functional form of the estimator will guarantee consistency even when a subset of the regression models used to generate the estimates are misspecified. In this work we construct DR estimators, following Bickel et al. (1993), Hines et al. (2021), and Kennedy et al. (2021) by deriving the efficient influence function (EIF). Influence functions are fundamental object in mathematical statistics and allow one to construct estimators with many favorable properties. In addition to the doubly robust feature described previously, estimators constructed using influence functions are asymp-totically normal, often achieve fast rates of convergence and allow researchers to integrate machine learning into estimation while still admitting traditional inference (point estima-tion, confidence interval construction and hypothesis testing).

## 5.3 Large sample properties 

The EIF in Theorem 2 is identical to that for the treatment specific survival curve in the concurrent population V˜a = 1 and so inherits the same asymptotic properties (West-ling et al. 2024). If the nuisance models required to estimate G, S, and π are correctly specified it can be shown that the resulting estimators \dRMST oc DR and \dRMST ac DR are root-n consistent, asymptotically normal and have valid 95% confidence intervals given by \dRMST oc DR ± pdvar {ϕoc (Z, dRMST )/n } and \dRMST ac DR ± pdvar {ϕac (Z, dRMST )/n }. In addition, both estimators admit a form of double robustness in that if either S or G and π

are estimated consistently then the resulting estimator will be consistent. 

## 5.4 On efficiency 

When estimators are based on parametric hazard regression, pooling can improve precision for the concurrent control estimand only when Assumption A7 ensures that the pooled and concurrent control hazards coincide (so pooling does not change the target) and the fitted hazard models are correctly specified (or otherwise converge to the true concurrent hazards). In this regime, the pooled likelihood uses a superset of the control risk-set contributions 20 at each time point, increasing Fisher information for the hazard parameters and thereby reducing the hazard-estimation component of the plug-in estimator’s asymptotic variance through the delta-method mapping from the model parameters to the targeted causal parameter; see Q4 in Section 10 of the Supplement. Outside this regime, pooling should be viewed as a bias–variance tradeoff relative to the concurrent estimand rather than as an efficiency improvement. These patterns are reflected in the simulation results (Figure 1 and Figure 4 in Section 6). The bottom row of Figure 2 (Section 6) shows that the DR estimator using all controls can improve efficiency when V˜a is stochastic given E. We now give a heuristic intuition for why this can occur. Any efficiency gains from pooling in the DR setting arise through a different mechanism than in parametric plug-in regression. Under Assumption A7 and when the availability indicator V˜a is stochastic given X = ( E, W )(so that 0 < P(V˜a = 1 | X) < 1 on a set of positive probability), nonconcurrent controls overlap with concurrent units at the same covariate values. In this overlap regime, pooled controls contribute valid information about the concurrent control nuisance functions (e.g., the control hazard and censoring hazard given X), and the EIF for the pooled estimator effectively replaces the noisy indicator weight 1{V˜a = 1 } by its conditional mean P(V˜a =1 | X) (a Rao–Blackwellization of the selection weight). This reduces the variance of the influence-function correction term and can yield a strictly smaller asymptotic variance when nuisance functions are consistently estimated. By contrast, if V˜a is deterministic in 

E, the V˜a = 0 controls are off-support for the concurrent covariate distribution and cannot improve efficiency in the nonparametric sense; any apparent gains then reflect additional modeling restrictions rather than information borrowed at the same X.21 6 Simulations

## 10.2 Q2: When is Assumption A7 plausible? 

Assumption A7 is a restriction on the true distribution P0 stating that, among control-arm participants who are event-free and uncensored through time m (i.e., Im = 1), the event hazard at time m does not depend on whether treatment ˜ a was available at entry, once we condition on ( E, W ): h(m, 0, e, w ) = h(m, 0, ˜a, e, w ) for all e ∈ E 1 and in its equivalent form Lm ⊥ V˜a (Im = 1 , A = 0 , E, W ). Thus, beyond the deterministic case V˜a = v˜a(E)where the assumption is redundant, A7 is plausible precisely when V˜a carries no residual information about the control event process after conditioning on the measured covariates 

W and entry time E.An important setting in which Assumption A7 may be implausible is when there exist unmeasured calendar-time or site-level factors that jointly influence treatment availability 39 and control outcomes. Let U denote generic unmeasured calendar-time or site-level features (e.g., changes in standard of care, diagnostic intensity, background risk, or enrollment practices) that may affect the event process. If U is associated with V˜a within levels of (E, W ) and also prognostic for the event hazard within the control risk set, then A7 will generally fail: 

U̸ ⊥ V˜a | (E, W ) and Lm̸ ⊥ U | (Im = 1 , A = 0 , E, W ) = ⇒ Lm̸ ⊥ V˜a | (Im = 1 , A = 0 , E, W ).

Since Assumption A7 is equivalent to Lm ⊥ V˜a | (Im = 1 , A = 0 , E, W ), the above implication suggests that there exist ( m, e, w ) (with e ∈ E 1 and P(Im = 1 , A = 0 , E =

e, W = w) > 0) such that 

P(Lm = 1 | Im = 1 , A = 0 , V ˜a = 1 , E = e, W = w)̸ =

P(Lm = 1 | Im = 1 , A = 0 , V ˜a = 0 , E = e, W = w),

and consequently, in the original hazard notation, 

h(m, 0, e, w )̸ = h(m, 0, ˜a, e, w ).

Conversely, A7 becomes more plausible when the observed covariates W (possibly together with E) adequately adjust for the drivers of both availability and baseline prognosis, so that any remaining association between V˜a and the control event hazard is negligible. 40 10.3 Q3: How does assumption A7 relates to parametric model specification?

# 16 Practical considerations 

What estimands should we target? Recent regulatory guidance (FDA 2021, ICH 2017) advocates first determining the causal estimand of interest through scientific discussion with subject matter experts before positing a statistical model for the outcome of interest. Only after the estimand and its necessary components (target population, outcome variable, etc.) are established do researchers proceed with selecting an optimal estimation strategy. The organization of this work and the target causal estimand follow the recommendations of (FDA 2021). 62 Integrating the shared control arm. Following the discussion in Section 10 we ad-vise against pooling concurrent and nonconcurrent controls. Combining control subjects requires a sequence of related assumptions which are not testable (random censoring) and are likely to be untenable in real world applications (correct model specification and haz-ard/censoring function stability over time). Simple numerical studies like those described in Section 6 show how pooling controls when Assumption A7 does not hold can invalidate the statistical analysis. 

Precision via stronger baseline predictors. Recent work suggests that incorporating stronger predictor variables can improve efficiency in estimating treatment effects (Colan-tuoni & Rosenblum 2015). In this article we propose this approach as a means to improve efficiency in place of integrating the complete study population of control subjects. Evi-dence for this alternative can be found in the real world data application Section 8, specifi-cally the improvement in efficiency obtained by OR oc and DR oc despite the fact that these estimators only incorporate concurrent control units. 

What estimators should we use? Assuming Model 1, to estimate dRMST we suggest employing the doubly robust estimator using only the concurrent control population de-noted DR oc . Since the estimator DR oc integrates only concurrent controls the previously mentioned difficulties associated with utilizing the entire shared control arm are not a factor in estimation. Researchers do not need to be concerned with the many challenges asso-ciated with pooling controls discussed in Section 10, and any estimation bias which may come as a result. Additionally, the estimator maintains the desirable properties discussed in Section 5.2, i.e. valid statistical inference and additional safeguards against model mis-specification compared to outcome regression based techniques. 63 Sample size and power calculations. Theory and results from the numerical studies in this work suggest an efficiency gain when including non-concurrent control subjects into the data analysis with outcome regression estimators. Although these results appear promising, we advise conducting sample size and power calculations as if only the concurrent control units were available. During the analysis of trial data, if researchers desire efficiency gains, non-concurrent control data may be included as described earlier, but with the understanding that the outcome model must be correctly defined, otherwise treatment effect estimates may be biased and the estimated 95% confidence intervals invalid.
