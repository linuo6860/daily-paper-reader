Abstract 

Cancer is often driven by specific combinations of an estimated two to nine gene mutations, known as multi-hit combinations. Identifying these combinations is critical for understanding carcinogenesis and designing targeted therapies. We formalise this challenge as the Multi-Hit Cancer Driver Set Cover Problem (MHCDSCP), a binary classification problem that selects gene combinations to maximise coverage of tumor samples while minimising coverage of normal samples. Existing approaches typically rely on exhaustive search and supercomputing infrastructure. In this paper, we present constraint programming and mixed integer programming formulations of the MHCDSCP. Evaluated on real-world cancer genomics data, our methods achieve performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods while running on a single commodity CPU in under a minute. Furthermore, we introduce a column generation heuristic capable of solving small instances to optimality. These results suggest that solving the MHCDSCP is less computationally intensive than previously believed, thereby opening research directions for exploring modelling assumptions.

## 1 Introduction 

Cancer remains a leading cause of mortality worldwide, with an estimated 9.7 million cancer-related deaths in 2022 [ 7 ]. This number is projected to rise to over 18 million in 2050 [ 25 ]. Understanding the biological mechanisms that drive cancer development is therefore essential. According to the multi-hit theory of carcinogenesis, multiple mutated genes (called hits) are required to cause cancer in humans [ 4 , 26 , 31 ]. Mathematical models estimate that the number of mutated genes (hit size) typically ranges from two to nine depending on the type of cancer [ 24 , 39 , 34 , 5, 3]. Despite this understanding, identifying the specific combinations of gene mutations responsible for a particular cancer remains a challenge. Discovering these gene combinations leads to a better understanding of carcinogenesis and enables the development of more effective targeted therapies [ 2, 11 ]. Although other factors, such as the tumor environment [ 29 ] and epigenetic modifications [ 30 ], also influence cancer development, this work focuses on identifying carcinogenic combinations of mutated genes. While several approaches aim to identify individual driver mutations [ 33 , 32 , 13 , 21 ], such gene mutations alone are generally insufficient to explain cancer development, of which several examples are provided by [ 11 ]. Another theory is based on the assumption of mutual exclusivity, which states that mutations in certain genes rarely co-occur and that a mutation in any of them may be sufficient to drive cancer. The identification of mutual exclusivity is done via so-called driver pathway discovery [ 35 ]. Based on this assumption multiple methods

Other methods focus on identifying modules of co-occurring cancer driver genes using biological interaction networks [ 20 , 38 , 38 ]. All of the aforementioned approaches differ in the modelling assumptions from multi-hit models, which aim to identify multi-hit gene combinations. The problem of identifying carcinogenic multi-hit gene combinations was first modelled as a weighted set cover by [ 11 ]. In their model, they require all tumor samples to be covered while minimising a weight that penalises gene combinations that also appear in normal samples. However, when evaluating the resulting solution via binary classification, misclassified tumor samples are not overly penalised. In the existing literature, this formulation of the problem is primarily addressed using exhaustive search-based approaches. These methods often begin by enumerating all or a large subset of possible gene combinations. The resulting weighted set cover problem is then solved using an iterative greedy heuristic, where in each iteration a gene combination is selected based on its weight. Once a gene combination is selected, all samples covered by that combination are marked as tumor samples. This process continues until all tumor samples are covered. These approaches have been applied to enumerate all gene combinations for 2, 3 and 4 hits [ 11 , 1 , 10 ]. A graph-based heuristic capable of identifying gene combinations of up to 8 hits was developed by [ 27 ]. A key limitation of existing methods is their substantial computational cost. The enumeration of all possible gene combinations typically requires parallelised algorithms executed on supercomputing infrastructure. For instance, one approach employed 6,000 V100 high-performance data center GPUs (reported as 48 million GPU cores) [ 10 ], while the current state-of-the-art method used 280 nodes on the Summit supercomputer (estimated 11,760 CPU cores) [ 27 ]. In contrast, our proposed methods can be run on a single commodity CPU while achieving similar performance. Constraint programming and mixed integer programming tools are well-known for solving set cover-type problems [ 6, 18 , 17 ], and have been widely used in constraint-based classification and pattern mining tasks [ 15 , 12 ]. In particular, optimisation-based techniques have been successfully applied to bioinformatics problems [16]. Since the identification of multi-hit gene combinations can be formulated as a binary classification problem, we relax the assumption that all tumor samples must be covered, allowing more flexibility in the model. If needed, this restriction can be easily enforced again. A subset of gene combinations induces a binary classification of samples: a sample is classified as a tumor if at least one selected gene combination occurs in it, or as normal sample otherwise. We propose an objective function that balances two criteria, namely maximising the number of correctly classified tumor samples (true positives) and minimising the number of times normal samples are misclassified as tumor (false positives). We formalise this problem as the Multi-Hit Cancer Driver Set Cover Problem (MHCDSCP). Note that for both the weighted set cover problem and the MHCDSCP it is not necessary to enumerate all possible gene combinations. Instead, the main goal is to identify which subset of gene combinations lead to the best classification performance. Our contributions are as follows. To model the MHCDSCP, we propose both a constraint programming and a mixed integer programming formulation, which take as input a set of candidate gene combinations. We evaluate these methods on real-world cancer genomics data. The mixed integer programming formulation achieves performance comparable to existing approaches and can be solved on a single commodity CPU within a minute. To potentially obtain optimal solutions, we also introduce a column generation framework, in which a pricing problem dynamically generates gene combinations as needed during the optimisation. We are able to solve small instances to optimality and demonstrate that the mixed integer R. S. H. Willemsen and T. Abeywickrama, R. Anandakrishnan 3

programming formulation provides near-optimal solutions. These findings indicate that the MHCDSCP, and its variations, are not as computationally demanding as previously believed. As a consequence, our models enable the evaluation of hypotheses in multi-hit cancer theory that were previously intractable. They make optimisation methods accessible to re-searchers working in cancer genomics. Our methods achieve similar classification performance to existing methods while identifying smaller sets of gene combinations. This has implications for the application of cancer detection, as it potentially strengthens the evidence for the multi-hit theory that a limited number of gene mutations drive the development of cancer. The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. In Section 2 we introduce the MH-CDSCP and provide its mathematical formulations. Section 3 details how these formulations can be solved within a column generation framework. The proposed methods are evaluated in Section 4 on real-world data. Finally, in Section 5 concludes and discusses directions for further research. 

## 2 Problem description 

In this section, we introduce the Multi-Hit Cancer Driver Set Cover Problem (MHCDSCP) as a binary classification problem. We discuss performance measures to evaluate the classification quality. Afterwards, we provide a constraint programming and mixed integer programming formulation for the MHCDSCP. 

## 2.1 Notation

## 2.2 Multi-Hit Cancer Driver Set Cover Problem 

In the MHCDSCP, we aim to select a subset of gene combinations C∗ ⊆ C that accurately classifies samples as tumor or normal. Selecting a set C∗ induces a binary classification of samples as follows. A sample s ∈ S is classified as tumor if at least one c ∈ C∗ covers s,and as a normal sample otherwise. The MHCDSCP seeks to identify a subset of gene combinations that balances two criteria, namely maximising the number of correctly classified tumor samples and minimising the number of times a normal sample is misclassified. Let T ∗ ⊆ T and N ∗ ⊆ N be the set of samples that are classified by the selected gene combinations C∗ as tumor samples. We define the number of true positives as |T ∗|, false negatives as |T \ T ∗|, false positives as |N ∗|, and true negatives as |N \ N ∗|. ## 3 Column generation framework 

The large number of gene combinations C makes it impractical to solve the mixed integer programming formulation (8)-(14) directly. Column generation is a well-established technique for addressing such large-scale problems by working with a restricted subset of variables and iteratively introducing new variables as needed [ 14 ]. We first present a relaxed version of the mixed integer program. We then introduce a fast heuristic and a column generation heuristic to provide bounds on the objective value. 

## 3.1 Restricted master problem 

In column generation, we start from a limited set of gene combinations ˜C ⊆ C, which may initially be empty. Instead of solving the mixed integer program (8)-(14), we solve a so-called restricted master problem, which corresponds to the linear relaxation of the mixed integer program restricted to gene combinations in ˜C.Solving the restricted master problem provides information in the form of dual values that can be used to generate gene combinations not contained in ˜C that may improve the objective value. Such gene combinations correspond to new columns and are added to ˜C in an iterative procedure. Repeating this process yields an optimal (possibly fractional) solution of the linear relaxation of the mixed integer program (8)-(14). The resulting objective value provides a valid upper bound on the optimal objective value of the original problem. ## 3.3 Column generation heuristic 

Instead of adding greedily generated columns, a column generation heuristic uses the dual values from the restricted master problem to generate promising columns. This allows us to efficiently explore the set of all gene combinations without the need for full enumeration. This method works as follows. First, the restricted master problem is solved and the dual values associated with the constraints are passed to the pricing problem. These dual values are used in the pricing problem to identify gene combinations (columns) with positive reduced costs. When these positive reduced cost columns are added to ˜C, these columns may improve the objective function. The processes of solving the restricted master problem and the pricing problem is repeated iteratively until no more gene combinations (columns) with positive reduced cost exist. At this point, an optimal solution to the linear relaxation of the mixed integer program is obtained, providing a valid upper bound on the objective value. In some cases, this solution is already integral, yielding a provably optimal solution to the original problem. ## 5 Conclusion 

Existing approaches to identify carcinogenic multi-hit gene combinations often rely on exhaustive search-based methods and require supercomputing infrastructure. In contrast, we introduce fast and practical methods that can be solved on a single commodity CPU within a minute. Specifically, we introduce a constraint programming and mixed integer programming formulation for the problem. In addition, we propose a column generation method, which allows us to solve instances to optimality and demonstrate that our mixed integer program provides near-optimal solutions. These results suggest that identifying these gene combinations is not as computationally intensive as previously believed. Since the problem turns out to be computationally tractable to solve, this opens up the possibility for other research directions. It becomes possible to test hypothesis in multi-hit theory that were previously intractable to solve. Since our proposed methods are flexible they can easily accommodate a range of practical assumptions, such as changing the weight of tumor and normal samples, requiring all tumor samples to be covered, varying the number of selected gene combinations, or fixing the size of each gene combination (hit range). These optimisation models become accessible to researchers in cancer genomics. For further research, it would be interesting to design an exact method guaranteed to solve the problem to optimality. A key bottleneck is the resolution of the pricing problem, which is currently formulated and solved as a mixed integer program.
