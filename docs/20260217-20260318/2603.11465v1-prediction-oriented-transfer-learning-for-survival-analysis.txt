## Yu Gu â1, Donglin Zeng 2, and D. Y. Lin 31Department of Statistics and Actuarial Science, University of Hong Kong, Hong Kong

Abstract 

Transfer learning is beneficial for survival analysis, especially when the target study has a limited number of events. However, existing transfer learning methods rely on the restrictive assumption that the target and source studies share similar parameters under Cox models, and most require access to individual-level source data. In this article, we propose a novel transfer learning framework that enhances model-based survival prediction by transferring predictive rather than distributional knowledge from source studies. Our approach employs flexible semiparametric trans-formation models for the target data while eliminating the need to model or share the source data. The ingeniously designed penalty enables simple and stable com-putation via an EM algorithm. We rigorously establish the asymptotic properties of the proposed estimator and show that it achieves a faster convergence rate than the target-only estimator when source knowledge is sufficiently accurate. We demon-strate the advantages of our methods through extensive simulation studies and an application to two major breast cancer studies. 

Keywords: EM algorithm; Nonparametric likelihood; Survival prediction; Transfer learning; Transformation models.

# 1 Introduction 

Transfer learning has been extensively studied in computer science over the past decade. By leveraging knowledge from related source studies, transfer learning can address challenges posed by insufficient samples and improve learning performance in the target study. It has been successfully applied to a wide range of real-world problems, such as natural language processing, image processing, speech recognition, biomedical research, and recommender systems (Zhuang et al. 2020). Recently, transfer learning has gained considerable attention within the statistics com-munity. Various methods have been developed to tackle statistical problems such as high-dimensional linear regression (Bastani 2021, Li et al. 2022, Gu et al. 2024), high-dimensional generalized linear models (Tian & Feng 2023, Li et al. 2024), functional linear regression models (Lin & Reimherr 2022), and Cox regression models (Li et al. 2023, Xie et al. 2024). Most existing methods incorporate source information into the target learning procedure via a penalty that encourages similarity between the modeled target and source distribu-tions, with the specific penalty determined by the underlying similarity assumption. In regression models, a typical assumption is that the Lr-distance ( r â [0 , 2]) between the target and source parameters is small, motivating the use of the Lr-penalty (Bastani 2021, Li et al. 2022, Tian & Feng 2023, Li et al. 2023, Xie et al. 2024). Alternatively, Gu et al. (2024) proposed an angle-based penalty assuming the angle distance between the target and source parameters is small. These methods fall into the category of distance-based transfer learning. Another category, representation transfer learning, assumes shared or similar low-dimensional representations between the target and source parameters (Tian et al. 2023, He et al. 2024). In this article, we explore transfer learning in the model-based prediction context of 2survival analysis, which holds important implications for medicine and public health. For example, studies of chronic diseases often face limitations due to a small number of events resulting from short study periods or low disease incidence rates. Relying solely on data from a single study can lead to unsatisfactory performance in risk evaluation and survival prediction. Transfer learning can enhance performance by using auxiliary knowledge from related studies with higher event rates or longer study periods. Another example is sur-vival analysis for underrepresented populations, such as racial and ethnic minorities and children, where transferring knowledge from majority populations can improve estimation and prediction performance on the target population. Despite its potential, the literature on transfer learning for survival analysis is quite limited. All existing methods are established under the Cox regression model, and none have theoretical justifications. Li et al. (2016) used an L2,1-penalty to learn a shared representation between the target and source studies. Li et al. (2023) and Xie et al. (2024) developed distance-based transfer learning methods for right- and interval-censored data, respectively, with L0- or L1-penalties applied to both the regression coefficients and the cumulative baseline hazard function. Lu et al. (2025) addressed covariate shift using density ratio weighting and aggregated the weighted partial likelihood functions from the target and source studies as the objective function. All these methods specify Cox models for both the target and source data and require that all studies have the same set of covariates. Thus, they are susceptible to model misspecification and are not optimal when the target and source studies have different sets of covariates, as only shared covariates can be used. Moreover, these methods impose the restrictive assumption that the (cumulative) baseline hazard functions are similar between the target and source studies, which can be easily violated in practice due to heterogeneities in study populations. Last but not least, the 3methods of Li et al. (2016) and Lu et al. (2025) require sharing individual-level source data, which is often infeasible due to privacy concerns. For instance, investigators typically cannot download individual-level data from large biobanks such as UK Biobank (Sudlow et al. 2015) and the All of Us Research Program (All of Us Research Program Investigators 2019). Similarly, individual-level data from major cohort studies such as the Atherosclerosis Risk in Communities (ARIC) Study (ARIC Investigators 1989) and the Womenâs Health Initiative (WHI) (Writing Group for the Womenâs Health Initiative Investigators et al. 2002), as well as from electronic health records across different health care systems, are generally not shareable, even in anonymized form, due to regulatory and institutional restrictions. To overcome the limitations of existing transfer learning methods for survival analy-sis, we propose a novel framework called Prediction-Oriented Transfer Learning (POTL). POTL aims to enhance model-based prediction by directly integrating predictive knowledge from source studies. Unlike existing approaches, which focus on transferring knowledge of model parameters, POTL is fully prediction-driven: the entire transfer learning procedure operates on survival predictions rather than on parameter sharing. This prediction-oriented feature distinguishes POTL from all existing methods. Moreover, transferring predictive knowledge requires imposing penalties directly on survival probabilities, which is compu-tationally far more challenging than penalizing individual model parameters. For example, even under simple Cox models, applying conventional Lr-penalties ( r â [0 , 2]) to sur-vival probabilities leads to intractable computation. We overcome this difficulty in POTL through a new penalization strategy and an efficient optimization algorithm. Our approach has several major advantages. First, unlike existing methods that require similar distributions or model parameters between the target and source studies, we impose 4a much weaker assumption that the studies share similar survival predictions. Thus, our approach remains applicable even when source models differ substantially from the target model and direct parameter transfer is infeasible. Second, we consider a broad class of semi-parametric transformation models with potentially time-dependent covariates for the target study, while leaving the models for all source studies completely unrestrictedâthey can be traditional regression models, machine learning models, or artificial intelligence (AI) mod-els such as large language models. This flexibility makes POTL highly robust to model misspecification and allows it to accommodate a wide variety of source models. Third, POTL does not require sharing individual-level source data, as only summary-level predic-tion information is transferred. Finally, we introduce a novel cross-entropy-type penalty that is computationally much easier than traditional Lr penalties. This computational advantage arises from our penaltyâs natural connection to the weighted log-likelihood of current status data, which enables simple and stable computation via an EM algorithm. We establish a rigorous asymptotic theory for transfer learning in survival analysis through the novel use of empirical process theory. Specifically, we show that the proposed estimator for the survival function converges to the true survival function at an optimal rate that is no slower than the standard n1/2 rate based on target data only. This theoret-ical foundation ensures that our POTL approach effectively leverages source information to enhance prediction performance in the target study. We evaluate the operating charac-teristics of our approach through extensive simulation studies and provide an application to the TCGAâBRCA (The Cancer Genome Atlas Network 2012) and METABRIC (Curtis et al. 2012) studies on breast cancer. 52 Methods

## 2.4 Asymptotic Theory 

In Web Appendix A, we establish the convergence rate of the proposed predictor ËS(t|X). Our theoretical results show that, when the source predictors are sufficiently accurate, POTL achieves a faster convergence rate for ËS(t|X) than the target-only method. More-over, we provide an explicit expression for the tuning parameter Îžn that guarantees the optimal convergence rate of ËS(t|X). In practice, standard data-driven procedures such as 12 cross-validation can be used to select Îžn to achieve good prediction performance in finite samples.

# 4 Application 

We apply the proposed method to breast cancer data from the TCGAâBRCA and METABRIC studies. The TCGAâBRCA study, which collected tumor samples from 1,096 patients, is designated as the target study, while the METABRIC study, comprising 2,509 patients, serves as the source study. Data from both studies are publicly available at https:// gdac.broadinstitute.org/ (TCGA-BRCA) and https://www.cbioportal.org/study/ summary?id=brca_metabric (METABRIC). Clinical variables assessed in both studies include age at diagnosis, number of positive lymph nodes examined, tumor stage, histological type, estrogen receptor (ER) status, progesterone receptor (PR) status, and human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2) status. We dichotomize tumor stages into early stage (stages 0, I, and II) versus advanced stage (stages III and IV), and we dichotomize histological types of breast cancer into invasive ductal carcinoma (IDC) versus others. # 5 Discussion 

In this article, we introduce a novel, prediction-driven transfer learning approach for sur-vival analysis, which leverages predictive knowledge from source studies to enhance survival prediction in the target study. Our approach stands out from existing methods by rely-ing on a less stringent similarity assumption and accommodating various types of source predictors, without the need to model source distributions or share individual-level source data. We provide theoretical guarantees by showing that the optimal convergence rate of our proposed estimator is faster than the target-only rate when the L2-distance between the target and pooled source survival functions is sufficiently small and the convergence rate of the pooled source predictor is not too slow. Our approach has shown satisfactory performance in both the simulation studies and the real data application. 21 In Section 4, we used a source study (i.e., METABRIC) with available individual-level data to enable direct comparison with CoxTL and pooled analysis, both of which require access to individual-level source data. However, our approach is broadly applicable to privacy-sensitive data sources that do not permit sharing of individual-level information, such as large biobanks, electronic health records, and major cohort studies. Furthermore, online risk calculators are increasingly used in clinical and research settings. These tools typically estimate 5- or 10-year risk of certain disease using models developed from one or more large cohorts. Examples include the widely used FRAX tool for estimating fracture risk, the National Cancer Instituteâs Breast Cancer Risk Assessment Tool (Gail model), and the American Heart Associationâs recently developed PREVENT calculator for estimating cardiovascular disease risk. Our approach can be used to transfer risk estimates from such online calculators, thereby leveraging information from large external cohorts to improve prediction performance in target studies with limited sample sizes. Our approach assumes that all K source studies contain useful predictive knowledge, which may not be the case as K becomes large. It is therefore worthwhile to develop methods for selecting informative source studies. One straightforward data-driven method is to evaluate the L2-distance between the target-only survival predictor and each source predictor ËSk(t|X) ( k = 1 , . . . , K ). Source predictors with distances from the target-only predictor below a certain threshold can be selected for transfer learning. Alternatively, transferable source studies can be identified through cross-validation, akin to the source detection algorithm proposed by Tian & Feng (2023). Currently, our transfer learning procedure employs a pooled source predictor across all 

K source studies. This might not be optimal, as the source studies might provide different survival predictors due to heterogeneity in their populations. Inspired by recent work by 22 Xiong et al. (2023) and Zhan et al. (2024) on linear regression models, we may consider a source-mixing structure for the target population. That is, the survival prediction of the target population is approximately a mixture of predictions from the K source populations. For each subject in the target population, we can identify the most relevant source pop-ulation and transfer knowledge from only the matched source predictor, rather than from all source predictors. This approach is particularly meaningful for cancer studies, where the target population may encompass latent demographic or biological subpopulations, necessitating precise transfer learning to optimize survival prediction performance.
